Gay Rights Supreme Court Timeline

The following timeline is a timeline of some of the most important and influencial court cases for the LGBTQ community.

  • One, Inc. v. Olsen

    1958 One, Inc. v. Olsen

    This court case was one of the first cases that considered LGBTQ rights concerned freedom of speech. The final decision of this case was that people had the right to publish LGBTQ media because there lifestyles and behavior weren't "inherently obscene".

  • Baker v. Nelson

    1972 Baker v. Nelson

    This court case considered, for the first time, the issue of marriage equality. This all started because a same-sex couple was reported for attempting to get married. John Baker, one of the men in question, enrolled in law school just to find a loophole. The Supreme Court decision was to ignore this case and it had little impact on the community. Later the two men were able to get married in Minnesota.

  • Bowers v. Hardwick

    1986 Bowers v. Hardwick

    This court case was a major blow to the LGBTQ rights movement when the court made the decision to uphold an outdated Georgia sodomy law. The decison of this court case gave the Gay community the identity of "criminal" for engaging in Gay acts and only furthered the negative stigma toward the community. This was also the time of the AIDS epidemic so the issues were only worsened.

  • Romer v. Evans

    1996 Romer v. Evans

    This court case was famous for overturning a past Colorado law, which didn't allowed cities to make anti-discrimination laws for people. The courts decision was ruled that laws couldn't single out LGBTQ people to take away their rights.

  • Boy Scouts of America v. Dale

    1996 Boy Scouts of America v. Dale

    This court case did the opposite of progress for the LGBTQ community. In 1990 the Boy Scouts of America expelled James Dale, an assistant scoutmaster and Eagle Scout, for being a leader in a university Lesbian/Gay Alliance. The courts decision was to rule in favor of the Boy Scouts of America because his homosexual conduct went against their values as an organization.

  • Lawrence v. Texas

    2003 Lawrence v. Texas

    This court case is famous for overturning the Bowers v. Hardwick case, which made sodomy illegal in some states. The courts decision to eliminate sodomy laws was a big turning point for Gay rights. The court recognized that the LGBTQ community are entitled to constitutional protections for intimate conduct.

  • United States v. Windsor

    2013 United States v. Windsor

    This court case was monumental in leading the way to marriage equality. The court made the decision to eliminate the portion of the Defense of Marriage Act of 1996, which stated that a legal union is between one man and one woman. This decision was one step closer to equality.

  • Obergefell v. Hodges

    2015 Obergefell v. Hodges

    This case is famous as the case that granted marriage equality to the LGBTQ community. It started and ended with a group of 14 same-sex couples who connected due to the connection of there partners passing away. They joined together and won the biggest victory for LGBTQ rights. The courts decision granted marriage rights, as well as any other benefits opposite-sex couples already experienced like social security and survivor benefits.

This section of the site focuses on three huge historical moments that many closely associate with the Queer community.

The StoneWall Riots

The stonewall riots began on June 28th, 1969 and ended on July 3rd, 1969. The riots were started at the historic Stonewall Inn in New York City and they began due to police brutality and unlawful search and seizure of a Gay owned, operated, and attended establishment. The Stonewall riots started due to this brutality and spanned multiple days. Many people credit this historic moment as the full start of the Gay Right movement, which is still an ongoing battle and will be for a while. What is a Pronoun & why it matters

In English, our most commonly used pronouns (he/she) specifically refer to a person’s gender. For queer, gender non-conforming, non-binary, and transgender people, these pronouns may not fit, can create discomfort, and can cause stress and anxiety.

Some Common Terms to Know:

  • Gender Identity:The internal perception of one’s gender, and how they label themselves, based on how much they align or don’t align with what they understand their options for gender to be.
  • SEX:Sex is a label, "male or female", that you’re assigned by a doctor at birth based on the genitals you’re born with and the chromosomes you have. It does not necessarily match someone’s gender / gender identity.
  • Cisgender:Applies to someone whose gender matches their “assigned” sex at birth.
  • Transgender:Applies to a person whose gender is different from their “assigned” sex at birth. Doctors typically assign gender based on sexual organs, but sex and gender are different.
  • Non-binary:Non-binary, similar to genderqueer, is a spectrum of gender identities that are not exclusively masculine or exclusively feminine‍—‌identities that are outside the gender binary of male and female.
  • Gender Fluid:Applies to a person whose gender identity changes over time or changes at different times.
  • Sexual Orientation:An emotional, romantic or sexual attraction to other people; of the same gender, different gender or multiple genders.

Human Rights Campaign

The Human Rights Campaign and the Human Rights Campaign Foundation together serve as America's largest civil rights organization working to achieve LGBTQ equality. By inspiring and engaging individuals and communities, HRC strives to end discrimination against LGBTQ people and realize a world that achieves fundamental fairness and equality for all. The Human Rights Campaign envisions a world where lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender and queer people are ensured equality and embraced as full members of society at home, at work and in every community.

The Trevor Project

In 1994, producers Peggy Rajski and Randy Stone saw writer/performer Celeste Lecesne bring to life Trevor, a character they created as part of their award-winning one-man show WORD OF MOUTH. Convinced Trevor’s story would make a wonderful short film, Stone and Rajski invited Lecesne to adapt it into a screenplay. Rajski directed the movie and TREVOR went on to win many prestigious awards including the Academy Award® for Best Live Action Short Film. In 1998 The Trevor Project was founded and is the leading national organization providing crisis intervention and suicide prevention services to lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer & questioning (LGBTQ) young people under 25.
